# Grammar Spec

This file is reserved for stable grammar summaries extracted by the architecture side channel.

## Scope

- Andrews PDF is the grammar-rule authority for engine architecture: order, roles, slots, boundaries, categories, dependencies, and supported derivational operations.
- Modern Nawat/Pipil orthography is the spelling/surface authority. Classical Nahuatl examples from Andrews must not be copied verbatim into Nawat output; they must be realized through Nawat/Pipil orthography and confirmed Nawat-specific exceptions.
- Andrews-supported derivations remain structurally valid after letter conversion. Classical `tl` maps to Nawat `t`, `tla-` maps to `ta-`, `tla-(pa-tla)` corresponds structurally to Nawat `ta-(pa-ta)`, Classical `(pa-ti)-tl` corresponds to Nawat `(pa-ti)-t`, and Classical person `-h` maps to Nawat `-t`.
- Nawat/Pipil repo evidence and user-provided forms decide lexical examples, spelling realization, and Nawat-specific exceptions; they do not demote Andrews to analogy for engine rules.
- Andrews is lesson-per-lesson, but the UI/engine is category-based. Lesson rows are curriculum/status and evidence indexes; implementation should extract reusable grammar categories, metadata, controls, and diagnostics rather than creating one engine or UI surface per lesson.

## Ordinary NNC Class/Source Contract

`nounClass` is a grammar class only:

- `t`: vowel-final stem, displayed as `(...V)t`.
- `ti`: consonant-final stem, displayed as `(...C)ti`.
- `in`: consonant-final stem, displayed as `(...C)in`.
- `zero`: consonant-final or vowel-final stem, displayed as `(...C/V)Ã˜`.

Data status is separate:

- Fixture-backed forms use source metadata such as `fixtureId`, `sourceRefs`, and `sourceKind: "fixture"`.
- Dynamic unconfigured stems use `openStem: true` and `sourceKind: "open-stem"`.
- `lexical` and `open` are not valid nounstem classes.

UI wording should keep these roles apart:

- Predicate class belongs with the predicate stem/composer.
- Subject affixes are shown as `ni...Ã˜`, `ti...Ã˜`, `Ã˜...Ã˜`, etc.
- Possessor belongs to predicate state.
- Nonanimate plural is reference/distributive behavior, not plural subject agreement.

## Ordinary NNC Formula Slots

The display formula is a diagnostic/teaching layer, not a generator.

Structured slot metadata comes first:

- `pers1Pers2.slot = "pers1-pers2"`: subject person prefix/suffix.
- `predicateStem.slot = "STEM"`: predicate stem, with predicate state kept in predicate metadata.
- `num1Num2.slot = "num1-num2"`: num1-num2 connector, conditioned by nounstem class.

The visible echo is derived from those slots:

- `#ni...Ã˜(tapiyal)Ã˜#`
- `#Ã˜...Ã˜(siwa)t#`

The final marker in the echo is the `num1-num2` subject-number connector slot. It is not a separate lexical suffix and not predicate state.

## Ordinary NNC Category Profile

`categoryProfile` is a derived explanation layer. It does not generate forms and does not replace `formulaSlots`.

Source-of-truth order:

1. Existing generation result fields.
2. `formulaSlots`.
3. Derived `categoryProfile`.
4. Rendered UI labels.

Derived categories:

- Predicate state: `absolutive` -> `absolutivo`, `possessive` -> `posesivo`.
- Possessive state: tracks whether possession was requested, which possessor prefix was requested, and whether possessive marking is available.
- Animacy: `animate` -> `animado`, `inanimate` -> `inanimado`; animacy constrains subject agreement and affects plural/reference behavior.
- Reference: `singular` or `plural`, with plural type retained as `count` or `distributive`.

UI labels should be short and explicit:

- `Estado del predicado: absolutivo`
- `Animacidad: inanimado`
- `Referencia: plural`
- `Conector num1-num2: Ã˜`

## Ordinary NNC Control Gating

UI controls are not grammar sources. They may inspect the current candidate result to avoid presenting unsupported slots as ordinary choices.

Control rules:

- Possessor selection is `Estado/poseedor`, not a promise that possessive marking is available.
- `Ã˜` means absolutive/no possessor and must remain available.
- Possessor-prefix choices are unavailable when the probed result says `categoryProfile.possessiveState.markingAvailable === false`.
- A requested unsupported possessor remains visibly selected so the diagnostic remains explainable.
- Class controls refer to the `Conector num1-num2` slot and only expose `t`, `ti`, `in`, and `Ã˜`.
- Fixture-fixed class or animacy locks conflicting UI choices.
- Animate plural controls are reference controls; inanimate plural-like behavior is distributive reference.

## Lessons 17-19 Supplementation And Topic Evidence Boundary

Lessons 17-19 (`suplementacion partes uno a tres`) are partially audited as sentence-layer metadata. None of these lessons is mapped into parser, search, generation, or visible UI behavior yet.

Structural frame from Andrews; Nawat/Pipil orthography and surfaces remain separate:

- Lesson 17.1 treats supplementation inside combinations of two or more nuclear clauses, contrasting it with adverbial modification over one nuclear clause.
- Lesson 17.2 defines supplementation as apposition whose head is always a personal pronoun.
- It relates a pronominal head to an adjoined nuclear-clause supplement or concatenated nuclear-clause group.
- Possible supplement roles include subject, object, and possessor.
- Lesson 17.3 shared-referent supplementation makes the supplement pronoun and principal-clause head point to the same referent.
- Lesson 17.4 requires contact by identity of personal-pronoun referents, not stem identity, and records recursive adjunction plus third-person subject/object ambiguity.
- Lesson 17.5 topicalization is preposed supplementation: the supplement before the principal clause is the topic, and following material is the comment.
- Lesson 17.6 information questions replace a supplement with an interrogative pronominal NNC in sentence-initial position.
- Lesson 18.1 integrated supplementation lets antecessive-order `o#` prefix to a preposed supplementary subject/object instead of to the VNC containing its head.
- Lesson 18.2 short personal-pronominal NNCs cannot stand alone as utterances; they ordinarily occur as supplements and may serve as principals only when adjoining other constituents.
- Lesson 18.3 marked supplementation uses `in` to subordinate a complete lower-level unit to a higher-level unit; `in` does not itself determine an NNC predicate.
- Lesson 18.4 discontinuous supplementation needs distance-aware head/supplement tracking, especially for possessor supplementation affected by topic movement.
- Lesson 18.5-18.7 record special agreement/referent cases: collective-like NNCs, named-partner supplements, and male-speaker `oquich` group supplements.
- Lesson 18.8 records silent specific object heads with `tla-(ayi)`: the VNC may look intransitive, but the supplementary object apposes a silent object pronoun.
- Lesson 18.9 deletes a principal VNC under limited conditions and upgrades an adverbial adjunct to proxy principal.
- Lesson 18.10-18.11 separate the so-called vocative from real vocative constructions; real vocatives involve third-person subjects, speaker-conditioned formation, and particle/prosody behavior.
- Lesson 18.12 makes free sentence-constituent order an interpretation problem centered on the principal clause, not a word-order generator.
- Lesson 19.1 extends supplementation to VNCs: intransitive and transitive VNCs can function as supplementary subject, object, or possessor, usually with optional `in`.
- Lesson 19.2 treats special plurals of `in`, `on`, and `ac` as supplementation structures with a VNC built on `(0-i-h)`, not as ordinary word plurals.
- Lesson 19.3 defines included-referent supplementation: the entire adjoined clause/sentence is the referent of a third-person-singular pronominal head in the principal.
- Lesson 19.3 also records principal-clause types and semantic groups for transitive principals: saying/questioning, causing, wanting/desiring/needing, perception, knowing/remembering/forgetting, and affect.
- Lesson 19.4 English infinitive renderings require included-referent supplementation, a future-tense adjoined VNC, and subject-coreference; translation is not an engine rule.
- Lesson 19.5 records fixed rumored report with `quil`, often with `mach`, as a diagnostic report structure.
- Lesson 19.6 records deletion of a principal clause of saying after speech-action VNCs, leaving direct or indirect speech juxtaposed without direct grammatical relation to the visible principal.
- Discontinuous supplementation and agreement mismatch require sentence-level metadata.
- VNCs can function as supplements structurally.
- None of these structures should be copied into Nawat/Pipil forms without local evidence.
